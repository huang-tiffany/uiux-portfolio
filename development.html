DEPLOYED SITE

Role Designer, Developer

Timeline Spring 2024, 3 weeks

Designing and implementing a functional aggregator for planning art and design museum trips in New York, with features decided based on competitor analysis of reference sites in adjacent industries.

01 The Problem

Though review and travel sites have existed for years, current databases house disparate sources of information in widely varying interfaces that make information gathering difficult. As such, I sought to design a single source of New York museum travel information, starting with its most famous destinations.

02 Competitive Analysis

Before designing, I analyzed Spotify, Pinterest, and Amazon as examples of different kinds of aggregators to determine the most important features to implement. Although the three sites had a lot of features in common, their designs differed slightly within how they balanced master lists and user-created lists.

01 Spotify
Despite lacking a “master list” for all saved songs, Spotify’s extensive filtering and organization, combined with its simplification of actions into streamlined icons, declutters the interface to highlight main actions for users to take.
02 Pinterest
Pinterest had more of a simplified layout, eliminating filters while using a tree layout and one-click saves to layer secondary actions under the most pertinent ones.
03 Amazon
Combining qualitative customer ratings with to quantitative metrics, along with the straightforward grouping and layout, makes Amazon’s design more robust while allowing users to do more without compromising visual clarity.
03 Wireframing Process

Although having a master list, one-click save, and view specs weren’t universal across the aggregators, they still seemed to be beneficial for the user experience, which is why I decided to implement these three functions as part of my website.

Starting with lofi mockups, I sketched various screens for how the user journey might look as users navigate through the site. Considering the interfacing of different menus, I also played around with page layout and how it might be affected by the opening and closing of different compartments on the page.

After some revision, I took the lofi mockups into a higher fidelity, adding color and font to determine the visual style of the pages. The designs changed throughout the development process as I discovered holes in my user flow and changed the designs to match potential user actions, but the core of the visual identity and experience stayed relatively consistent.

04 Functionality

The final interface details various museums in New York for visiting or learning about art history. The two buttons in the top left corner allow for viewing two different aggregators, one for a master list (favorites), and another for a more specific list (save for later).

Within these lists, users have the ability to switch between grid and list views of the data. The three menus employ similar design principles so they can be grouped visually and experienced similarly in terms of the visual language, actions, and motion implemented.

Within each card displayed on the page, users can view information and visit links to the museum sites upon hovering, as well as add and remove cards from both lists. A filter menu, opened via the icon in the bottom left, allows for users to simultaneously sort by museum ticket price and filter based on area and genre. The number of results, like the number of favorited or saved items, is displayed in the filter menu for consistency and to show when there may be no results returned. All of the menus have the ability to clear filters or aggregated items.

05 Final Insights

Overall, even though it was a difficult process to navigate through the different hooks while keeping track of all the moving parts of my application, I thought it was an informative way of translating market research into stronger products. Additionally, finding the most visually appealing way of displaying data while creating a fluid and satisfying experience is an ongoing challenge, but it was a fulfilling way to learn how translating designs into code could be applied to real-life projects.
