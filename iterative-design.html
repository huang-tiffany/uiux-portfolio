Working with Zest, a corporate gifting start-up, alongside a team of designers to develop interface changes accommodating a new shipping feature for their bulk ordering option.

01 The Problem

Our design team had the privilege of working with Zest, a corporate gifting startup, to develop a user flow for a new bulk shipping feature, which would allow customers to ship multiple orders to a single address (as opposed to shipping to multiple addresses).

02 Sketches

To begin, each team member brainstormed two end-to-end flows for possible implementations of a bulk shipping process. Although some elements remained consistent in the high-level information hierarchy, others — like the visual display of the various shipping options — varied from sketch to sketch.

While brainstorming, a few questions arose.

01 Per-Recipient UI
Since users who ship to one address are likely bulk ordering (e.g. for 100+ people) and likely won’t want to personalize gifts, are per-recipient UI elements necessary?
02 Enabling Bulk Shipping
How should we enable bulk shipping? Ideas included drop-down menus, a simple checkbox, and a third box in the delivery method section.
03 Shopping Carts
Is a shopping cart necessary? How will it interact with the rest of the user flow?
03 Lofi Wireframes
After sketching, we proceeded to make lofi wireframes to narrow down our designs before sharing with the client, paying special attention to new additions along with the elimination of unrelated UI elements.
01 Delivery Methods
Placing the delivery method under the “ship to recipients’ addresses” option as a dropdown and third option groups the two shipping options while keeping the third virtual gift option separate.
02 Sticky Order Summary
Adding a sticky order summary with more specific language clearly communicates the total prices after shipping and reduces back-and-forth between checkout and order pages.
03 Individual Addresses
Removing addresses from the recipients list allows for a more streamlined workflow and eliminates an unnecessary step for bulk shipping.
04 Peer and Advisor Critique

We then participated in a critique with around 20 other students in our studio to get unbiased feedback (since none were familiar with Zest prior) and also met with our advisor, Vanessa Cho, for a midpoint check-in.

From the critique, we learned that we should analyze competitors and include sketches to show our entire design process. On the design side, some specific feedback we received included making the delivery method boxes more uniform and making it easier to switch between pages of the gift options.

While speaking with Vanessa, she called attention to our copy (specifically the phrase “ship all to one address”) and suggested examining the language used by Zest’s competitors, in addition to minimizing extra clicks in the user flow.

05 R1 Client Feedback

After implementing this feedback, we recorded a Loom to present our first draft to our client, Christine Pun, Founding Designer at Zest. Most of her questions pertained to alternative ways of displaying information, such as why we broke our shipping methods into two instead of three. We realized that we chose this primarily for aesthetic reasons and ultimately reverted to employing one box per shipping method since it clarified the differences between the various options better.

06 Hifi Prototype

Because there were no major design changes to the wireframe, we proceeded to make the high-fidelity prototype, as Zest requested a mobile prototype to see what the order summary would look like on a smaller screen.

We integrated our minor design changes, including changes in copy and distinct delivery options, directly in our high-fidelity prototype.

07 Style Guide

Using Zest’s existing branding based on their hifi Figma screens, we used Milk Bar as the in-situ brand as the foundation for our style guide, noting that Zest tends to use their clients’ colors for greater personalization and brand identification.

With a sans serif typeface without styling like Centra2 for readability, we kept the text size consistent across mobile and desktop to allow for easier implementation of our design on multiple devices. Adding to the original style guide, we included a few more icons, created an option to remove cart items on hover, and added a custom calendar when selecting a delivery date.

08 R2 Client Feedback and Revision

Finally, we presented our hi-fi prototype to Christine and Jeremy, a co-founder of Zest. Along with discussing possible A/B tests in the future to ground the order summary design and having the option to change the number of recipients in the order summary, we also talked through the order of the page sections and how that can affect the user flow.

After our meeting, we took their advice into account and added an input field to the order summary, since allowing users to edit the number of recipients as they order made more sense for the entire flow. However, we also set the default number of recipients to 1 so that the original totals would be easier to understand at first glance. This would also update as users change the number of recipients in the “Recipients” page section during the main page flow to stay consistent with the general flow.

09 Final Insights

Working with a team in a UI/UX setting was surprisingly smooth, likely due to the fact that all of the team members had a background in design. Being able to receive feedback from a real client was also an informative process, which allowed us to contextualize our own design process in a real working environment.
